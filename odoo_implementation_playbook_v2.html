Section 01
The Philosophy

Before touching a single configuration — or a single prospect — you need to internalize what Odoo actually believes about implementation. This is the "why" that governs every decision on every project.

The One-Sentence Version

A project is successful when it goes live on time and on budget. Everything else — customer satisfaction during the project, custom features, early upsells — is secondary. Common sense always prevails over any rule.

The 3 QuickStart Principles
01
Stay Standard

Every deviation from standard increases complexity exponentially — not linearly. Adapt the customer's process to Odoo, not Odoo to the customer's process. The platform is flexible; the methodology is firm.

02
Deliver Fast

Speed over customization keeps projects on schedule and reduces risk. A fast Go-Live builds momentum and trust. The longer a project drags, the more costly and risky it becomes — for everyone.

03
Keep It Affordable

Custom development carries ~25% annual maintenance cost. Affordable, fast implementations win more business, generate referrals, and build the long-term relationships that drive genuine upsell.

What Is — and Isn't — a Priority
✅ What Actually Matters

Getting users onto Odoo on time and within budget. Budget overruns and timeline slippage are the two reasons projects fail — full stop. A delivered, imperfect system beats a delayed, perfect one every time.

❌ Common Traps to Avoid
  • Custom development as a priority before Go-Live
  • Using customer satisfaction as your KPI mid-project
  • Upselling additional services before the project goes live
  • Importing full data history when master data suffices
  • Writing documentation for the customer instead of training them to write it
  • Accepting scope creep mid-cycle without dropping something else
Why Customer Satisfaction Isn't the Goal Mid-Project

Customer satisfaction fluctuates constantly throughout a project. Key-users want more features; the CEO wants on-time and on-budget — these goals directly conflict. A Project Leader who optimizes for mid-project satisfaction risks losing the project itself. Temporary dissatisfaction from a tough conversation is far better than a missed deadline. Periodic ratings are used to identify customers who need more attention — not to grade Project Leaders.

The Long Game on Upselling
Why We Don't Upsell Before Go-Live

Every time you upsell before Go-Live, you weaken trust. Customers may think twice before buying future services. A bloated first project destroys the relationship that enables long-term revenue. Focus on success first — upsell follows naturally from satisfied customers.

The Better Strategy

Deliver fast. Build trust. Then upsell from strength. It's 7× easier to sell to an existing happy customer than to acquire a new one. Phase non-essential features after Go-Live — this protects margins, reduces project risk, and creates a natural future engagement cycle.

Phase 1 · ROI Analysis
Validate feasibility, define scope & budget

Sold before the customer commits to the full project on larger engagements. Ranges from 3 to 50 days depending on complexity. On smaller projects, this is performed during Kick-Off. At this stage the BSA is still a salesperson — the project isn't won yet. Historical data imports must be reviewed by the Pre-Sales team or BSA Manager before being included in scope.

  • Be a salesperson — reassure and motivate, run live demos of key features
  • Observe how they currently work; current state matters more than future goals
  • Never present multiple options — propose one best solution and let them challenge it
  • Classify items as "optional" yourself; don't let the customer make that call
  • Peer-review with an App Expert before presenting results to the customer
  • All development must be classified: must-have before Go-Live, or post Go-Live

Phase 2 · Project Kick-Off
Align everyone, set expectations, build a real plan

The highest-leverage phase. The entire project's success depends on how well you execute it. You're generating buy-in, setting honest expectations, and aligning everyone on the Odoo methodology — not the customer's preferred methodology. If the planning looks too short, negotiate now. This is where you earn the right to lead the project.

  • SPoC formally understands and commits to the methodology
  • Expectations set honestly — including what will be difficult
  • Project plan finalized with realistic, agreed-upon timelines
  • SPoC begins learning Odoo immediately via eLearning
  • Onboard the customer's project team in the change strategy
⚠️
Don't delay hard conversations. Inexperienced PLs avoid difficult discussions during Kick-Off — this is among the most common causes of late-stage project failure. Tackle misalignment early or it compounds.
80%of project
Phase 3 · Implementation
Build, configure, validate, and train in short weekly cycles

The project moves forward in repeating short cycles — analyze, configure, test, train, import, launch. The solution is shaped progressively. The SPoC is engaged at every step. Maintaining a steady pace is the single biggest success factor — keep the SPoC engaged in everything and momentum will follow.

  • Configuration: BSA configures directly including Studio; SPoC validates through sessions
  • Data Import: Master data only; avoid full history; never delay Go-Live for data quality
  • Custom Dev: Must-haves only before Go-Live; BSA writes every spec; SPoC attests conformity
  • Have the SPoC drive the keyboard during training — not just observe
  • Don't accept mid-cycle change requests unless another requirement is dropped
  • Never implement something you're not convinced of — a contract is less important than project success
5%of project
Phase 4 · Go-Live
Put it in production — fast, even if imperfect

Issues at Go-Live are normal. Your job is to react quickly, not to prevent all issues. Never push back the Go-Live date. The risks of delay almost always outweigh the risks of going live with known issues — pushing the deadline by months exposes the project to extra costs, motivation loss, and new change requests that weren't on the table before.

  • Training is not a conference — have users perform the flows themselves
  • Create momentum: make Go-Live something the team anticipates and celebrates
  • React to issues quickly — problems are acceptable if fixes are fast
  • Be on-site day one if there is strong user resistance to change
  • Check days later that users are in Odoo — not reverting to old software out of habit

Phase 5 · Second Deployment & Progress Report
Expand scope based on real production experience

About one month after Go-Live, review the deferred development backlog. Typically 50% of planned development is no longer needed once users are in production — and better-informed new requests emerge that the customer couldn't have anticipated during planning. The Progress Report engages top management using the Digital Opportunities Matrix: Quick Wins, Game Changers, Fine Tuning, and what to Avoid. This is where organic, trusted upsell happens naturally — and why doing the first project right is so important.

Section 04
The Process Cycle

Within the Implementation phase, the team works in repeating short cycles. Each cycle follows the same six-step sequence, delivering tangible progress every week and keeping the customer engaged and accountable.

Step 01
Analyze

Analyze business workflows to determine configuration strategy. Ask "why," not just "what." Always understand the current state before designing the future state.

Step 02
Configure

Configure the workflow using Odoo standard features. If unsatisfactory after testing, return to analysis — not to custom development as a first resort.

Step 03
Test & Validate

SPoC and key users test the workflow and validate for business use. If unsatisfactory, return to analysis — not to a growing backlog of exceptions.

Step 04
Train Users

Train SPoC and key users on validated workflows. SPoC drives the keyboard — not just watches. Training is hands-on practice and active participation, never a presentation.

Step 05
Import Data

SPoC gathers and prepares data files. Import master data only. Don't delay Go-Live waiting for perfect data — post-production cleaning is acceptable and common.

Step 06
Launch

Go live on the cycle's scope. Begin using workflows daily. Troubleshoot issues with the BSA immediately as they arise. Fast reaction beats perfect preparation.

Digital Opportunities Matrix

Used in the Progress Report phase to identify and prioritize what comes next. Every opportunity is plotted by potential impact and ease of transformation.

🚀 Quick Wins
High impact + easy to implement. Top priority. These build momentum and deliver visible ROI fast — do these first.
🎯 Game Changers
High impact + complex to implement. Handle after quick wins. These transform the business but require real planning and commitment.
🔧 Fine Tuning
Low impact + easy. Nice to have. Schedule when bandwidth allows — these are never a priority over higher-impact work.
🚫 Avoid
Low impact + complex. No real benefit justifies the cost. Decline confidently — this is a service to the customer, not a failure of ambition.

These are the operational rules that govern what can and cannot be sold as a QuickStart project. Every salesperson must know these before a deal is signed — not after.

🚨
Non-Compliant Projects: If a project does not meet these guidelines, the BSA Manager presents the issues to the Sales Manager, who takes over until the project is either refunded or restarted with aligned expectations. There are no exceptions.
Must-Have Requirements to Qualify a Project
  • Project meets the minimum requirements displayed on the Pricing Page — check apps and service type per pack
  • Packs of 25 hours or less are sold with the explicit understanding they are typically insufficient for a full implementation
  • Sales has detailed a clear scope of what will be completed with the purchased hours — this prevents scope creep and underselling

  • Customer is hosted on Odoo.sh or SaaS — this applies to all packs

  • SPoC is identified, committed, and has real decision-making authority
  • SPoC's required time commitment increases in relation to BSA hours — if BSA dedicates 5 hrs, SPoC may dedicate 10 hrs

  • If a customer goes on hold or abandons the project for 1+ month, they re-enter the queue at current lead time; re-engagement transition time is billed to the customer

Third-Party App (3PA) Policy by Hosting Type

The hosting environment determines what third-party apps are allowed and what Odoo will support. This has a direct impact on what can be promised during the sale.

SaaS

The most restrictive environment. eCommerce connectors (Shopify, WooCommerce, Magento) are not allowed. Any third-party app is not allowed on SaaS — only outside connectors (apps installed on the external system, not on Odoo) are permissible. Custom development is not deliverable on SaaS.

Odoo.SH — Odoo-Owned Repo

eCommerce connectors (Shopify, WooCommerce, Magento) are allowed; LoC maintenance not required, but Odoo developers will not bug-fix or upgrade them, and BSA cannot provide ongoing support for them. Third-party apps approved through code review are allowed with LoC maintenance and full bug-fix support. Rejected 3PAs are not allowed.

Odoo.SH — Client-Owned Repo

Most permissive but most technical. eCommerce connectors behave the same as Odoo-owned repo. Any third-party app is technically allowed regardless of code review, but Odoo developers will not bug-fix or upgrade them and BSA cannot provide ongoing support. Requires a very technical client — or passing to a partner.

📌
Custom developments are only deliverable on Odoo.SH. If Studio will be used in an advanced way (calculated fields, automated actions, report customizations) or if custom development is anticipated, the client must be on a major version of Odoo or Odoo.SH before the project starts.
Section 06
Implementation Challenges

Every project has predictable friction points. These aren't surprises — they're patterns. Knowing them in advance means you navigate instead of being blindsided.

🔄
How to Handle User Resistance to Change
+

Humans are deeply resistant to change — from the newest employee to the founder. Never sideline resistant people. Invest time in them. A change is always perceived as a cost and a risk. People will accept that cost only when the benefit is clearly, concretely larger. Don't dismiss the difficulty — acknowledge it, then sell the benefit.

The Product
Once users see features that directly benefit their daily work, acceptance increases rapidly. Tailor demos to their specific pain points. Don't demo generic flows to a resistant accountant — demo bank reconciliation.
The SPoC & Key Users
Their positivity and credibility within the company is your most powerful change management asset. A skeptical SPoC will poison the well. A champion SPoC will turn resistors into advocates. Invest in making them a true champion before Go-Live.
Executive Sponsorship
The CEO visibly supporting the project changes the dynamic entirely. Secure this during Kick-Off and remind the sponsor of their role when resistance escalates. A CEO who supports the project publicly saves implementations that would otherwise be abandoned.
🛑
How to Handle Custom Development Requests (4 Tactics)
+

Custom development costs approximately 25% of its original build cost every single year in maintenance and upgrades. Project complexity grows exponentially with each customization — not linearly. Remember the 80/20 rule: no more than 20% of project hours on development. Run all four challenges before agreeing to anything:

Tactic 1 · Is It Really Necessary?
Did the customer have this feature in their old software? If not, they can live without it for now. Recommend going live first and reassessing after production. Customer priorities almost always change once they're actually using the system.
Tactic 2 · Is It Worth Supporting the Cost?
Evaluate real benefit (hours saved per month) vs. the real cost (build + 2–3× ongoing maintenance over 5 years). Customers typically see only the one-time dev cost. Help them see the true total cost of ownership before they commit.
Tactic 3 · Is the Gain Significant Enough?
Question the volume. If a feature saves 10 minutes but applies to only 2 cases per month, a 10-day development is never justified. Quantify the time saved vs. the time invested before writing a line of code.
Tactic 4 · Can We Serve the Same Need Differently?
There is almost always a standard workaround, a simpler configuration, a Studio solution, or a third-party service that solves the problem in hours rather than weeks. Always explore this path first before writing a specification.
💡
When you convince the customer to avoid a development, explain exactly why. The more they understand your reasoning, the more they trust your judgment — and the more valuable your relationship becomes long-term.
📊
How to Manage Customer Expectations
+

Experienced Project Leaders set hard expectations upfront — not to discourage the customer, but to ensure that when things get difficult (and they will), the customer doesn't abandon ship. Promise less, deliver more.

What to Tell Them Upfront
This project will be difficult. There will be problems. But at the end, your company will be better for it. And I need you — as CEO and sponsor — to support the project when your teams complain, rather than folding to internal resistance.
What Never to Say
Never say "everything will go smoothly." Never say the change will be easy. Never set timelines that are too short just to close the deal. The short-term win creates a long-term problem for the project, the customer, and the relationship.
🧠
How to Keep Things Simple
+

Customers naturally add complexity. Your job is to strip it out — relentlessly. Simplicity is not a stylistic preference; it is a risk reduction strategy. The simpler the implementation, the more likely it succeeds on time and on budget.

Never Give Multiple Options
When you present three options, customers spend days deliberating and tend to choose the most complex one. Propose the best solution. Let them challenge it. But you make the recommendation and own the decision.
Force Decisions in the Moment
Prevent customers from defaulting to "let me check with the team" on every small decision. Delay creates compounding debt. Push for answers in the moment whenever possible — indecision is its own form of scope creep.
Use App Expert Peer Reviews
An external App Expert can make tough calls without political baggage. Bring them in at critical phases like GAP Analysis — they'll cut complexity that you've become too close to challenge on your own.
🏛️
How to Deal with Internal Politics
+

When things go wrong, people look for someone to blame. Internal politics is a game where everyone loses — including the project. The best strategy is to refuse to play. Focus entirely on what moves the project forward.

Don't Build a Defense. Solve the Problem.
Nobody cares whose fault it is as much as they care about what you're going to do about it right now. Focus on moving forward — even if the issue technically isn't yours. The project's success is always your responsibility.
Get Key-User Buy-In Before Starting
Projects imposed from above — where the people using the software weren't consulted and didn't choose it — generate resistance that can derail everything. Identify and address this dynamic during Kick-Off, not three months in.
In Conflicting Situations, the SPoC Decides
When different employees at the client's organization give conflicting information, the BSA carries out only what the SPoC has defined as a must-have. The SPoC's responsibility is to gather stakeholders, challenge requests, and define priorities — not to pass every conflict back to the BSA.
📝
How to Write a Good Specification
+

When custom development is truly necessary and approved, the specification is the contract between the BSA and the developer. A poor spec wastes time, creates rework, and erodes trust on both sides. Keep it short, visual, and structured.

Section 1 · Business Need
The use case (what) and the specific reason this feature is necessary (why). Maximum 2–3 paragraphs. Zero technical language. If the business need can't be explained in plain language, it's not well understood yet.
Section 2 · Functional Specification
The suggested solution using Odoo (how). Illustrated, wherever possible, with screenshots or mockups with annotations. Visual communication beats verbal description every time.
Section 3 · Technical Hints
Things the developer specifically needs to watch for: edge cases, integration dependencies, data considerations, and known limitations. This section saves the most time during development.
Section 07
Objection Handling

By understanding the root causes of common objections and mastering proven rebuttal strategies, you can confidently guide clients toward a successful implementation — even when they're skeptical or resistant.

The Feel-Felt-Found Method

The Feel-Felt-Found method is the foundation of effective objection handling. It builds trust, provides social proof, and leads the customer toward a positive resolution — without dismissing their concern or being defensive.

1
Feel

Acknowledge the customer's concern directly and demonstrate genuine empathy. Don't minimize it — validate that their worry is reasonable and understandable.

2
Felt

Share that other customers — especially those in similar situations — have felt exactly the same way. Normalize the concern. They are not alone in having it.

3
Found

Explain what those customers ultimately discovered: the concern resolved itself, the outcome was positive, and the decision to move forward was the right one.

Example in Practice

Customer: "I'm worried the implementation will disrupt our business."

You (Feel): "I completely understand how you feel. Implementing a new system can seem overwhelming at first, especially when you're running a busy operation."

You (Felt): "Many of our clients initially felt the same way — especially those with complex operations like yours."

You (Found): "What they found, however, was that our phased implementation process allowed them to stay fully operational while transitioning to Odoo gradually. They were often surprised by how smooth the process actually turned out to be."

Cost-Related Objections
💵
"The implementation is too expensive."
+
Root Concern

Budget constraints or doubt about the perceived value of implementation services relative to their cost.

Rebuttal

Focus on the long-term ROI and cost savings the implementation will provide. Odoo's subscription is approximately 7× lower than competitors' public prices — the implementation investment is often recovered within the first year through operational efficiency gains alone. Frame the cost as an investment with a measurable return, not an expense.

📏
"We can't justify the cost for a business of our size."
+
Root Concern

Doubt that the implementation will provide sufficient ROI for a company at their scale.

Rebuttal

Highlight the scalability of the solution and how implementation can be scoped and phased to match the business's size and budget. Encourage starting with just the essential 2–3 apps that solve their biggest pain points. It's easier to grow into the platform than to implement everything at once — and significantly less risky.

💳
"Why do we have to pay for the whole implementation before we go live?"
+
Root Concern

Lack of trust or concern about paying upfront without seeing the full value delivered yet.

Rebuttal

Explain the upfront costs associated with project planning, resource allocation, and BSA assignment. Work begins immediately upon payment — not at Go-Live. Hours are consumed throughout the entire project lifecycle, from analysis through post-Go-Live support. The structure protects both sides by ensuring the BSA's time is committed and available.

Root Concern

Concern about incurring costs during the lead time before active implementation begins.

Rebuttal

Clarify that the lead time refers to when active BSA-led work begins — but project planning and resource allocation begin immediately upon payment. Payment initiates the queue for BSA assignment. This is standard practice and protects the customer by ensuring they have a dedicated BSA waiting for them when the project kicks off.

+
Root Concern

Concern about paying for unused services and not receiving fair value for the investment.

Rebuttal

All sales are final — there are no refunds, including timesheets. Explain how hours are allocated across the project lifecycle, including post-Go-Live support. Having remaining hours after Go-Live is actually a benefit — those hours are available for the Second Deployment phase, additional training, or ongoing support. Unused hours are never truly wasted.

Timeline & Resource Objections
⏱️
"The implementation will take too long and disrupt our operations."
+
Root Concern

Fear of prolonged downtime or resource strain during the transition to a new system.

Rebuttal

Emphasize the phased implementation approach: users continue in their current system while Odoo is configured alongside it. There is no "big bang" cutover. Each phase delivers working functionality in short cycles, minimizing disruption to daily operations. The business stays operational throughout.

👥
"We don't have the internal resources to manage this project."
+
Root Concern

Lack of a dedicated team or the internal expertise to support the transition alongside daily responsibilities.

Rebuttal

The BSA drives the entire implementation — the customer doesn't need a project management team. What they need is a committed SPoC who can act as the internal point of contact and champion. The BSA provides the planning, the guidance, the configuration, and the training. The SPoC provides the business knowledge and internal authority.

📆
"Our business is seasonal and we can't risk downtime during peak periods."
+
Root Concern

Concern about the timing of the implementation conflicting with peak business periods.

Rebuttal

The phased approach makes scheduling flexible. Work with the customer to map out their seasonal calendar and plan Go-Live dates around peak periods. Configuration and training can happen during slower periods; Go-Live can be timed precisely. This is a planning conversation, not a blocker.

Product & Process Objections
🔗

+
Root Concern

Fear that their current software stack won't work well with Odoo.

Rebuttal

Discuss Odoo's native integration capabilities and available connectors. However, be clear about integration policy: Odoo does not build integrations for features already available natively in Odoo. For eCommerce, Shopify, WooCommerce, and Magento connectors are supported. For other integrations, explore whether the functionality already exists in Odoo before committing to custom connector development.

😤
"We've had a bad experience with ERP implementation in the past."
+
Root Concern

Lingering mistrust from previous failed ERP implementations or painful transitions.

Rebuttal

Acknowledge their past experience directly and without dismissing it — bad ERP implementations are common, and their concern is valid. More than 50% of proprietary ERP implementations fail industry-wide. Then differentiate: Odoo's 95%+ success rate exists precisely because of the QuickStart methodology, which was developed to address the exact failures that plague traditional implementations. The phased approach, the single BSA model, and the emphasis on staying standard are all direct responses to why ERP projects typically fail.

Support & Customization Objections
🛠️
"What if we need more customization than Odoo can handle?"
+
Root Concern

Doubt about Odoo's flexibility and adaptability to their unique business needs.

Rebuttal

Odoo is highly flexible — the key distinction is between customization (configuring Odoo's existing features to fit the business, which is unlimited) and custom development (building entirely new functionality). The former is always available; the latter is used strategically when genuinely necessary. Most business needs that feel unique can be addressed through smart configuration, Studio, or workflow adjustments — without touching code.

🎧
"What if we need post-implementation support? Do we have to keep paying?"
+
Root Concern

Concern about being left without adequate help once the system goes live.

Selling the Methodology

The methodology isn't just how we implement — it's our biggest competitive differentiator. Understanding how to position it in a sales conversation is as important as being able to execute it on a project.

The Core Sales Truth

Odoo's #1 differentiator is transparency. Most competitors obscure their pricing, methodology, challenges, and legal terms. Odoo is openly transparent about all of them. Counterintuitively, this transparency is your most powerful closing tool — the more honest you are upfront, the greater your chances of winning the business.

Odoo's Competitive Differentiators
💵
Transparent Pricing
~7× lower than competitors' public prices. Always say "inexpensive" — never "cheap." Inexpensive signals high value for low cost.
🖥️
Demo on Day One
Free 14-day trials and demos on the very first call. Most enterprise vendors won't show the product until month three of evaluation.
📋
Open Methodology
We publish and share our implementation approach. No black box. Customers know exactly what they're buying before they sign anything.
⚡
Speed to Value
95%+ implementation success rate vs. the industry average of under 50%. What competitors take 18 months to deliver, we deliver in 3–6.
📣
Honest About Challenges
We tell customers implementation is difficult before they sign. This builds more trust than promises of an easy transition — and it protects the project.
The 5 Sales Commandments
01
Start Small, Grow Big
It's easier to sell 2 apps than 10. And once you've delivered 2 apps successfully, selling 10 becomes natural. Encourage customers to start with their core need and expand. Starting small reduces their risk, reduces your risk, and creates a natural long-term engagement cycle. It's also easier to sell — and faster to close.
02
Control the Buying Process
Know who is involved in the decision, what they need to see to be convinced, and how they perceive risk. Ask transparently — customers respect this directness. If you don't understand their buying process, you have blind spots that will cost you the deal. Qualify the buying process as aggressively as you qualify the requirements.
03
Don't Let Requirements Grow Legs
Start and end every call by reinforcing the customer's primary requirements. If the list becomes contaminated with unnecessary items, cut it back and refocus. Requirement creep in pre-sales kills deals — customers experience it as uncertainty and risk. The salesperson who keeps the scope focused is the one who closes.
04
Sell Out-of-the-Box. Challenge Dev Requests.
Inexperienced salespeople are afraid to push back on customer requests in pre-sales. But customers generally respect being challenged — it signals expertise and confidence. Every development request added pre-sale increases the customer's perceived risk and uncertainty. Remember the 80/20 rule: more than 20% development on a project is a red flag, not a feature.
05
Cut Down the Number of Decision-Makers You Deal With Directly
When too many people feed you requirements and dictate the agenda, you can't separate priorities from noise. This results in significant effort with little progress. Focus your communication on the actual decision-maker. More stakeholders in the conversation doesn't mean more clarity — it means more confusion, longer cycles, and a lower probability of closing.
Selling by Company Size
Small Companies

Get them in front of the product on the first call — ideally in the very first conversation. Your goal is to show them what the standard solution does and demo the specific features they care about. Show Odoo Studio for minor customizations. Discuss ROI on the fly. The sooner you can demonstrate value, the more QuickStart projects you'll close — and close quickly.

Large Enterprises

Same principles — demo ASAP, keep it simple — but structured in distinct stages: (1) Get them to want Odoo as a product first (RFIs, demos). (2) Only then, sell the ROI Analysis. (3) Then sell the full implementation. You're selling yourself as a business partner, not just a product. Getting software in front of them on day one of evaluation is a differentiator — most competitors won't do it.

What Great Salespeople Know
The Demo is the Close

The key success factor in selling Odoo is mastering the product demo. Sales people who do 3+ demos per week consistently outperform those who don't. Master 3 generic flows first (service, manufacturing, and one other) — then adapt with experience.

Decision Makers vs. Key Users

Decision makers want a low budget and fast delivery. Key users want more features. Your goal is to satisfy the decision maker. When requirements pile up from key users, redirect the conversation back to the primary business objectives of the person who actually signs the contract.

Complexity Kills Deals

The #1 variable that jeopardizes any sale is complexity. Too many requirements, too many stakeholders, too many development requests, too many cooks — all of these add perceived risk. Your job is to simplify the path to "yes," not to accommodate every request on the way there.
