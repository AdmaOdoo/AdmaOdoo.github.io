Section 01

The Philosophy

Before touching a single configuration, you need to internalize what Odoo actually believes about implementation. This is the "why" that governs every decision on every project.

The One-Sentence Version

A project is successful when it goes live on time and on budget — everything else, including customer satisfaction during the project, custom features, and early upsells, is secondary.

The 3 QuickStart Principles
📦
Stay Standard

Odoo offers wide customization options — but every deviation from standard increases complexity exponentially, not linearly. The goal is to adapt the customer's process to Odoo, not Odoo to the customer's process.

⚡
Deliver Fast

Prioritizing speed over customization keeps projects on schedule and reduces risk. A fast Go-Live creates momentum. The longer a project drags, the more it costs, and the more that can go wrong.

💰
Keep It Affordable

Tailor-made solutions dramatically increase project costs — both upfront and ongoing. Custom development carries ~25% annual maintenance cost. Affordable projects win more business and generate loyalty.

What Is — and Isn't — a Priority
✅ What Actually Matters

Getting users onto Odoo on time and within budget is the only real measure of success. Budget overruns and timeline slippage are the two reasons projects fail — full stop. A delivered, imperfect system beats a delayed, perfect one every time.

❌ Common Traps to Avoid
  • Custom development as a priority before Go-Live
  • Using customer satisfaction as your KPI mid-project
  • Upselling services before the project goes live
  • Importing full data history when master data suffices
  • Writing documentation for the customer instead of training them
Why Customer Satisfaction Isn't the Goal (During Implementation)

Customer satisfaction fluctuates constantly throughout a project. Key-users want more features; the CEO wants it on time and on budget — these are different, often conflicting goals. A Project Leader who optimizes for satisfaction mid-project risks losing the project itself. Temporary dissatisfaction — from a tough conversation or a refused feature — is far better than a missed deadline. We use periodic ratings to identify customers who need more attention, not to grade Project Leaders.

The Long Game on Upselling
Why We Don't Upsell Before Go-Live

Every time you upsell before Go-Live, you weaken the customer's trust. They may think twice before buying services in the future. A failed or bloated first project destroys the relationship that makes long-term revenue possible.

The Better Strategy

Deliver fast. Build trust. Then upsell from a position of strength. It's 7× easier to sell to an existing happy customer than to acquire a new one. Phase non-essential features after Go-Live — this protects margins and creates natural future engagement.

Core Principle: Fewer People, Faster Decisions

Odoo is simpler than traditional ERPs — simple enough that one person can understand both the business and the product simultaneously. This is a structural competitive advantage. Too many cooks spoil the broth.

Phase 1 · ROI Analysis
Goal: Validate feasibility, define scope & budget

For larger projects, sold before the customer commits to the full project. Ranges from 3 days to 50 days depending on complexity. On very small projects, performed during Kick-Off. This is where the Project Leader is still a salesperson — the project isn't won yet.

What Gets Delivered
  • Analysis of savings and benefits (Returns)
  • Budget and implementation plan (Investments)
  • Mapping of business needs to product features
  • Proof of concept demos of key business flows
  • Change management strategy
Key Tips During This Phase
  • Be a salesperson — reassure and motivate, do live demos
  • Observe how they currently work (don't just ask future goals)
  • Never present multiple options — propose one best solution
  • Classify items as "optional" yourself; don't let customers decide
  • Peer-review with an App Expert before presenting results
  • All development must be classified as: must-have before Go-Live OR deployable after
5% of project
Phase 2 · Project Kick-Off
Goal: Align everyone, set expectations, build a real plan

This is arguably the most leverage-heavy phase. The whole project's success depends on how well you execute it. You're generating buy-in, managing expectations upfront, and aligning everyone on the Odoo methodology — not the customer's preferred methodology.

Critical Outcomes
  • SPoC understands and commits to the methodology
  • Expectations are set honestly — including the hard parts
  • Project plan is finalized
  • SPoC starts learning Odoo immediately
  • Change management strategy is activated
⚠️
Don't avoid hard conversations during Kick-Off. If the timeline looks too short, negotiate now. If there's misalignment on features, address it immediately. Inexperienced Project Managers delay difficult discussions — this is a top cause of late-stage project failure.
80% of project
Phase 3 · Implementation
Goal: Build, configure, validate, and train in short cycles

The project moves forward in short weekly cycles — analysis, configuration, development (if needed), validation, and training. The solution is shaped progressively. The SPoC is engaged at every step. Momentum is everything here.

The Three Streams
  • Configuration: PL configures directly, including Studio; SPoC validates through training sessions
  • Data Import: Import master data only; avoid full history; don't delay Go-Live for imperfect data
  • Custom Dev: PL decides what's worth building; only "must-haves" before Go-Live; PL writes specs, SPoC attests business conformity
Key Tips
  • Have the SPoC drive the keyboard themselves — not just watch
  • Transform your project plan into a series of quick wins
  • Don't accept new change requests mid-cycle unless another requirement is dropped
  • Never implement something you're not convinced of — a contract is less important than project success
5% of project
Phase 4 · Go-Live
Goal: Put it in production — fast, even if imperfect

Issues at Go-Live are normal. They happen because the database wasn't fully tested or because users forgot their training. Your job is to react quickly, not to prevent all issues. Never push back the Go-Live date — the risks of delay almost always outweigh the risks of going live with known issues.

  • Training is not a conference — have users do the flow themselves
  • Create momentum: make Go-Live something the team is eager for
  • React quickly to issues — it's okay to have problems if you fix them fast
  • Be on-site day one if there is strong user resistance
  • Check days later that they're actually using Odoo and not reverting to old software
+ post Go-Live
Phase 5 · Second Deployment & Progress Report
Goal: Expand scope based on real production experience

About one month after Go-Live, the PL reviews the backlog of deferred development. This is where the real conversation about expansion happens. Typically, 50% of planned development is no longer needed once users are in production — and new, better-informed requests emerge.

The Progress Report is a strategic tool for engaging top management — not just the SPoC. It uses a Digital Opportunities Matrix to identify what's next: Quick Wins, Game Changers, Fine Tuning, and items to Avoid. This is where organic upsell happens naturally.

Section 04

The Process Cycle

Within the Implementation phase, the team works in repeating short cycles. Each cycle follows the same sequence, delivering tangible progress every week and keeping the customer engaged.

The Implementation Cycle · Repeats Weekly
Step 01
Analyze
Analyze business workflows to determine configuration strategy. Ask "why" not just "what." Understand current state before designing future state.
Step 02
Configure
Configure the workflow to meet business needs using Odoo standard features. If the result is unsatisfactory, return to analysis — not to custom development.
Step 03
Test & Validate
SPoC and key users test the workflow and validate it for business use. If unsatisfactory, return to analysis and configuration — not the backlog.
Step 04
Train Users
Train SPoC and key users on validated workflows. SPoC drives the mouse. Users teach users. Training is not a lecture — it's hands-on practice.
Step 05
Import Data
SPoC gathers and prepares data files. Import master data only. Don't delay Go-Live for imperfect data — some cleaning can happen post-production.
Step 06
Launch
Go live on the cycle's scope. Begin using workflows daily. Troubleshoot issues with the BSA immediately as they arise. Fast reaction beats perfect preparation.
Support Structure
During Implementation
  • Implementation Consultant (BSA): Primary contact, functional expert, solutions architect, training lead
  • Developer: Custom dev and technical questions only, time & materials
  • App Expert: Peer review at critical decision points
Post Go-Live
  • Account Manager: Billing, payments, contract questions
  • Functional Support: Generic software questions and bug reports, 24/5 and free
  • Implementation Consultant: Available for expansion phases, training, coaching
The Digital Opportunities Matrix

Used during the Progress Report phase to identify what to tackle next. Every opportunity is plotted across two dimensions: potential impact and ease of transformation.

🚀 Quick Wins
High impact + easy to implement. Top priority. Move on these first — they build momentum and visible ROI fast.
🎯 Game Changers
High impact + complex. Handle after quick wins. These transform the company but require planning and commitment.
🔧 Fine Tuning
Low impact + easy. Nice to have but not priority. Can be done when bandwidth allows.
🚫 Avoid
Low impact + complex. No real benefit here. Decline these confidently — the cost exceeds the value.
Section 05

Implementation Challenges

Every project has predictable friction points. These aren't surprises — they're patterns. Knowing them in advance means you can navigate them instead of being blindsided by them.

🔄
How to Handle User Resistance to Change
+

Humans are deeply resistant to change — from the newest employee to the founder. Never put resistant people aside. Do the opposite: invest time in them. A change is always perceived as a cost and a risk. People will accept that cost if the benefit is clearly larger.

Don't tell them it's not risky or that it's "just a small change." Acknowledge the difficulty — then sell the benefit. Organize demos tailored to their pain points. If they can see the value, they'll accept the risk.

Your resources for change management:

The Product
Once users see features that benefit them directly, acceptance increases rapidly. Train resistant users on the specific things that will make their daily work better.
The SPoC & Key Users
Their positivity and credibility within the company is your most powerful change management tool. The SPoC's internal reputation matters enormously.
Executive Sponsorship
The CEO visibly supporting the project changes the dynamic completely. Secure this during Kick-Off and remind the sponsor of their role when resistance escalates.
🛑
How to Handle Custom Development Requests (4 Tactics)
+

Custom development costs ~25% of its original build cost every year in maintenance and upgrades. Project complexity grows exponentially with customizations — not linearly. Before agreeing to anything, run through all four of these challenges:

Tactic 1 · Is It Really Necessary?
Did the customer have this feature in their old software? If not, they can live without it for now. Recommend going live without it and reassessing after production — priorities almost always change once users are live.
Tactic 2 · Is It Worth Supporting the Cost?
Evaluate the real benefit (hours saved per month) vs. the real cost (development + 2-3× in ongoing maintenance over 5 years). Customers typically only see the one-time dev cost — help them see the full picture.
Tactic 3 · Is the Gain Significant Enough?
Question the volume. If the feature saves 10 minutes but only applies to 2 cases per month, a 10-day development is never worth it. Quantify before building.
Tactic 4 · Can We Serve the Same Need Differently?
There is almost always a standard workaround, a simpler configuration, or a third-party service that solves the problem in hours instead of weeks. Always explore this before writing a line of code.
💡
If you convince the customer to avoid a development, tell them why. The more they understand the reasoning, the more they'll trust your recommendations — and the more valuable your relationship becomes.
📊
How to Manage Customer Expectations
+

Experienced Project Leaders set expectations aggressively upfront — not to discourage the customer, but to ensure that when things get hard (and they will), the customer doesn't abandon ship. If you promise less, you can over-deliver.

What to Tell Them Upfront
This project will be difficult. There will be issues. But at the end, your company will be better. And I need you — as a CEO/sponsor — to support the project when your teams complain, not to fold.
What Not to Say
Never say "everything will go smoothly." Never say the change will be easy. Never set deadlines that are too short just to win the project. The short-term win creates a long-term problem.
🧠
How to Keep Things Simple
+

Customers naturally add complexity. Your job is to strip it out — relentlessly. Simplicity is not a style preference; it's a risk reduction strategy.

Never Give Multiple Options
When you present 3 options, the customer will spend days deliberating and then choose the most complex one. Propose the best solution. Let them challenge it — but you make the recommendation.
Force Decisions
Prevent customers from saying "let me check with the team" on every small decision. Delay creates debt. Push for answers in the moment whenever possible.
Use App Expert Peer Reviews
An external App Expert can make tough calls without political baggage. Involve them at critical phases like GAP Analysis — they'll often cut complexity that you've become too close to challenge.
🏛️
How to Deal with Internal Politics
+

When things go wrong, people look for someone to blame. Internal politics is a game where everyone loses — including the project. The best move is to refuse to play.

Don't Build a Defense. Solve the Problem.
When something fails, nobody cares whose fault it is as much as they care about what you're going to do about it. Focus entirely on moving forward — even if the issue isn't yours to own.
Get Key-User Buy-In Before Starting
Projects imposed from above — where the people using the software didn't choose it and weren't consulted — create resistance that can derail everything. Identify and address this dynamic during the Kick-Off.
📝
How to Write a Good Specification
+

When custom development is truly necessary, the specification is the contract between the PL and the developer. A bad spec wastes everyone's time and creates costly rework.

Section 1 · Business Need
The use case (what) and why the customer specifically needs this feature. Maximum 2-3 paragraphs. No technical language.
Section 2 · Functional Specification
The suggested solution using Odoo (how). Use screenshots or mockups with annotations. Visual beats verbal every time.
Section 3 · Technical Hints
Things the developer specifically needs to watch out for. Edge cases, integration points, data considerations.
Section 06

Selling the Methodology

The methodology isn't just how we implement — it's our biggest competitive differentiator. Understanding how to position it in a sales context is as important as being able to execute it.

The Core Sales Truth

Odoo's #1 differentiator is transparency. Most competitors obscure their pricing, methodology, challenges, and legal terms. Odoo is openly transparent about all of them. Counterintuitively, this transparency is your most powerful closing tool. The more honest you are upfront, the greater your chances of winning the deal.

Odoo's Competitive Differentiators
💵
Transparent Pricing
~7× lower than competitors' public prices. Always say "inexpensive" — never "cheap." Inexpensive = high value for low cost.
🖥️
Free Demo Day 1
Free 14-day trials, demos on day one. Most enterprise vendors won't demo until month 3. This alone is a differentiator.
📋
Transparent Methodology
We publish and share our implementation approach. No black box. Customers know what they're buying before they sign.
⚡
Speed to Value
95%+ success rate vs. industry average of less than 50%. Projects that others take 18 months to deliver, we deliver in 3-6.
📣
Honest About Challenges
We tell customers implementation is hard before they sign. This builds more trust than empty promises.
The 5 Sales Commandments
01
Start Small, Grow Big
It's easier to sell 2 apps than 10. And once you've sold 2 and delivered well, selling 10 becomes natural. Encourage customers to start with their core need and expand. This reduces their risk, your risk, and creates long-term engagement.
02
Control the Buying Process
Know who is involved in the decision, what they need to see, and how they perceive risk. Ask transparently. If you don't understand how they buy, you have blind spots. Qualify the buying process as aggressively as you qualify the requirements.
03
Don't Let Requirements Grow Legs
Start and end every call by reinforcing the customer's primary requirements. If the list becomes contaminated with unnecessary items, cut it back. Requirement creep in pre-sales kills deals — customers experience it as uncertainty and risk.
04
Sell Out-of-the-Box. Challenge Dev Requests.
Inexperienced salespeople are afraid to challenge customers in pre-sales. But customers respect being challenged — it signals expertise. Every development request added pre-sale increases the customer's perceived risk. Risks kill deals.
05
Cut Down the Number of Decision-Makers You Deal With Directly
When too many people feed you requirements, you can't separate priorities from noise. Focus your communication on the actual decision-maker. More people ≠ more information. It means more confusion and longer cycles.
Selling by Company Size
Small Companies

Get them in front of the product on the first call — ideally in the first conversation. Your goal is to show them what the standard solution does and demonstrate key features they care about. Show Odoo Studio for minor customizations. The sooner you can demo and discuss ROI on the fly, the more QuickStart projects you'll close.

Large Enterprises

Same principles — demo ASAP, keep it simple — but structured differently: (1) Get them to want Odoo as a product first. (2) Sell the ROI Analysis. (3) Then sell the full implementation. You're selling yourself as a business partner, not just a product. Get software in front of them on day one of evaluation — most competitors won't do this.
